10 Tips to Pass the Danish Theory Test on the First Try

The Danish theory test has a reputation for being tough — especially for people who've driven in other countries. Here's how to avoid the most common pitfalls and pass on your first attempt.

1. Don't Rely on Driving Experience Alone

This is the number one mistake, especially among expats who've been driving for years. The Danish theory test isn't about whether you can drive — it's about whether you know Danish traffic rules specifically. Rules about right of way, speed limits, and signalling are different in Denmark compared to the UK, US, or other countries.

Even if you've been driving for 20 years, study the Danish rules as if you're learning from scratch.

2. Master the Right-of-Way Rules

Right of way (vigepligt) is the topic that fails the most people. The key rules to memorise:

  • The right-hand rule: At unmarked intersections, you must give way to traffic coming from your right
  • Yield signs (shark teeth): The triangle yield sign and the shark-tooth road markings mean you must give way to all traffic on the road you're entering
  • Priority roads: The yellow diamond sign means you're on a priority road — you have right of way at the next intersection
  • Exiting roundabouts: When leaving a roundabout, you must yield to cyclists in the bike lane on your right

Pro tip: In the test, always check for cyclists. Denmark has extensive bike lanes, and many questions test whether you'd check for cyclists before turning right.

3. Learn the Danish Speed Limits by Heart

You'll be asked about speed limits repeatedly. The defaults are simple but absolute:

  • 50 km/h in built-up areas — look for the white city name sign
  • 80 km/h on rural roads
  • 130 km/h on motorways (110 km/h on some stretches)

Watch for signs that override the default. And remember: when you pass a city name sign with a red line through it, you're back to the 80 km/h rural limit.

4. Do at Least 20 Full Practice Tests

The single most effective preparation method is taking full-length mock tests under real conditions. Set a 25-minute timer and answer all 25 scenarios. This builds familiarity with the test format and helps you manage your time.

5. Study the Signs, Not Just the Rules

Many questions show a traffic scenario with specific signs visible. If you don't recognise the sign, you can't answer the question. Pay special attention to:

  • Signs that look similar but mean different things (e.g., no parking vs. no stopping)
  • Supplementary signs (small rectangular signs below main signs that add conditions)
  • Zone signs (e.g., 30 km/h zone) — they apply until you see the end-of-zone sign

6. Read Every Sub-Question Carefully

The test is designed to trick you with careful wording. A question might say "You must stop" vs. "You may proceed" — one word changes the entire answer. Read each statement twice before answering.

Also pay attention to negatives: "You do not need to signal" is easy to misread under time pressure.

7. Don't Rush — You Have More Time Than You Think

25 minutes for 25 scenarios might sound tight, but most people finish with time to spare. Take 30–60 seconds per scenario. Look carefully at the image, identify all vehicles, signs, and road markings, then answer the sub-questions.

If you're unsure about a question, make your best guess and move on. You can have up to 5 wrong answers and still pass.

8. Focus on Your Weak Topics

After a few practice tests, you'll notice patterns — maybe you consistently get roundabout questions wrong, or you struggle with parking rules. Spend extra time on those specific topics.

9. Learn the Danish Context

Even if you take the test in English, the scenarios are set on Danish roads. Understanding Danish road culture helps:

  • Cyclists are everywhere — always check bike lanes before turning
  • Headlights must be on at all times, even during the day
  • Denmark uses right-hand traffic (drive on the right)
  • Pedestrians at unmarked crossings generally have priority
  • The "2-second rule" for following distance is standard

10. Take the Test When You're Ready, Not When You're Rushed

Don't book the test until you're consistently scoring 20+ correct out of 25 on practice tests. The test fee is 600 DKK per attempt, and rebooking wastes time. It's much cheaper to spend another week practising than to pay for a second test.

Common Mistakes That Fail People

Based on patterns from thousands of test-takers:

  • Forgetting to check for cyclists when turning right — this catches people from countries without strong cycling culture
  • Confusing the right-hand rule with rules from their home country
  • Not knowing what supplementary signs mean — the small signs below main signs
  • Misreading "must" vs. "may" in question statements
  • Underestimating the test and not practising enough because they can already drive
